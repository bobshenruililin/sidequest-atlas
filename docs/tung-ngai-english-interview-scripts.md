**Frame from email:** background → achievements → aspirations.  
**Service spine:** Wu Zhi Qiao + Gannan; Laidlaw research as prerequisite for service; upcoming LiA (makesense Americas / Colombia / Global South).  
**Soft identity:** do not open with Shanghai-born / Hong Kong-raised.

Speak naturally. These are speakable targets, not essays.

## Q1 — Tell us about your background

I study Global Health and Development at HKU. My background is practical: I keep putting myself where public health is not abstract — in community service, peer teaching, and Mainland rural programmes.

Through Wu Zhi Qiao I’m a student coordinator for rural health and infrastructure work in Gansu. Before that, Gannan Horizons fieldwork — listening to retired steelworkers in Wudu, seeing a Tibetan temple function as a medical centre, hearing the grassland restoration story of the three bags of seeds — changed how I understand service. You cannot serve well if you do not first understand how people live and whom they trust.

So my background is not a list of activities. It is a habit: listen first, then act.

---

## Q2 — Achievements you want us to know

If I pick one thread, it is connecting evidence to service.

Academically, my Year GPA is 4.24 out of 4.30. Through Laidlaw, with Professor Bishai’s team, I analyse heat and cardiovascular admissions using Hospital Authority data across many months. The point is dual-season hospital preparedness and better surveillance thinking — so older people are protected before harm becomes acute.

On the service side, Wu Zhi Qiao is the achievement I care about most right now, because it turns that discipline into Mainland rural responsibility. Peer mentoring at HKUMed, Food Angel and Refugee Union volunteering, and STEM teaching assistant work keep me accountable to people, not only datasets.

I’m proudest not of titles, but of one consistent standard: research should make service more honest.

## Q3 — Aspirations / long-term goals

Long term, I hope to work in a senior public-health policy role at the Centre for Health Protection, focused on cross-boundary disease control with Mainland CDC counterparts.

Near term: deepen epidemiology and policy training; finish the Laidlaw research into a usable policy brief; serve seriously through Wu Zhi Qiao; and complete Leadership in Action with makesense Americas — planned climate-health community mobilization in Colombia / Global South settings — so I learn how communities organise around climate-health risks, not only how cities analyse them.

If I later pursue a JD after undergraduate study, it would be regulatory and common-law training for health policy — not a departure from public health, and not a Year-1 transfer.

Because the Foundation’s standard matches the person I’m trying to become: academic seriousness, affection for Hong Kong, cultural confidence about China, and public service that lasts longer than a CV cycle.

I don’t want funding without accountability. I want a community that expects Mainland engagement, scholar mentoring, and return of capacity to Hong Kong. That pressure is useful.

**(Community complement.)** I grow fastest when support comes with a responsibility to contribute — MIT Node, labs, Wu Zhi Qiao interns, mentors, conference communities all rhymed with that. What none of them fully gave was one long-term home connecting those lessons to Hong Kong and China. That is what I see in Tung & Ngai: a community of collaborative endeavour.

## Q5 — Aren’t you too research-heavy? Where is the service?

Fair question — and I heard a similar emphasis from HKU’s selection process.

I don’t treat research and service as opposites. In public health, **research is a prerequisite for responsible service**. Blind goodwill can waste resources or misunderstand a community.

Wu Zhi Qiao is where that becomes concrete: rural health and infrastructure require humility and coordination, not slogans. Local volunteering — Food Angel, Refugee Union, peer teaching, STEM TA — keeps me in contact with people. Laidlaw gives me the analytical discipline. Upcoming LiA with makesense Americas will test whether I can mobilise communities around climate-health using evidence from Hong Kong in a Global South context.

So the thread is: evidence → listening → service → return.

---

## Q6 — Wu Zhi Qiao / Gannan — what did you do / learn?

I’m a student coordinator with Wu Zhi Qiao for the rural health and infrastructure programme connected to Gansu. The coordinator role is about making a student team useful: logistics, listening, translating needs into feasible work, and keeping the group humble.

The Gannan Horizons fieldwork is what prepared me for that responsibility. Three moments stay with me. Retired steelworkers in Wudu who built a bumper-car space for local children and then rode with us — dignity and reinvention in ordinary places. A Tibetan temple that also functioned as a medical centre — health systems live inside culture. And the grassland restoration story of the three bags of seeds — local knowledge is expertise even without credentials.

What I learned: curiosity is not enough. Service needs restraint, respect for boundaries, and attention to particular people — not abstract “development.”

## Q7 — How does Laidlaw research serve society?

It asks whether heat risk for cardiovascular admissions has changed in a warming, ageing Hong Kong. Earlier studies suggested limited heat effects; newer conditions may differ. If risks are stronger, that should change warnings, hospital surge planning, and preparedness across seasons.

I want that work to become a policy brief on dual-season hospital preparedness and cross-boundary heat-health surveillance. That is service through readiness: protecting vulnerable people before the emergency ward fills.

Research is not the destination. It is how I earn the right to propose action.

---

## Q8 — Colombia LiA / makesense Americas — how does it connect?

It is **upcoming / planned** as Laidlaw Leadership in Action with makesense Americas — climate-health community mobilization and South-South health learning. I will not speak about it as completed work.

The link to Hong Kong and China is not copy-paste. Hong Kong gives me subtropical heat-health evidence in an ageing dense city. Colombia / Global South settings teach how communities organise, build trust, and mobilise around climate-health risks. I want those methods to improve how I serve later in Hong Kong, the GBA, and Mainland partnerships — as mutual learning, not export of one city’s model.

---

## Q9 — Hong Kong’s role in GBA / China’s public health

Hong Kong can be a trusted interface: Chinese and international, bilingual, common-law, close to the Mainland. Ageing and climate stress are continental-scale problems. Innovation is needed in interoperability, transparency, and cultural translation — so disease data, qualifications, and emergency powers can move with patients and pathogens.

At Shenzhen Bay in 2020, that seam was visible. That seam is where I intend to work.

---

## Q10 — If awarded, how will you better Hong Kong society?

Three commitments.

1. Continue service with discipline — Wu Zhi Qiao, peer teaching, community volunteering — and use Foundation opportunities for Mainland/GBA public-health exposure.  
2. Turn Laidlaw evidence into practical preparedness thinking Hong Kong hospitals and communities can use.  
3. After LiA, bring South-South mobilisation lessons home; mentor future scholars; present an HK–Mainland interoperability proposal at the scholar forum.

I will treat the award as a responsibility, not a prize.

## Q11 — Why not clinical medicine?

I respect clinicians deeply. My questions are system-level: preparedness, surveillance, cross-boundary coordination, and public trust. Clinical care saves the patient in front of you; public-health service changes the conditions under which many patients and institutions act. CHP sits closer to those questions.

---

## Q12 — Many activities — where is the depth?

Fair concern. I judge depth by whether activities return to one question: how trust in public health is built before systems can serve.

Wu Zhi Qiao and Gannan = field depth. Laidlaw = analytical depth. Peer teaching and community volunteering = service practice. Upcoming LiA = community-mobilisation practice. I am also learning that leadership includes saying no so the line stays clear.

I will be fully transparent with the Foundation and HKU. If concurrent awards are restricted during T&N tenure, I will comply. Laidlaw supports a specific research and leadership pathway; Tung & Ngai is a broader formation in Hong Kong–China service. I will not treat funding as an entitlement or double-count costs.

## Q14 — China’s rise (no slogans)

I avoid slogans. China’s scale means its public-health capacity, ageing policy, and trust systems affect huge numbers of people — and the region around it. Hong Kong can contribute because of proximity, bilingual capacity, common-law institutions, and international credibility. Cultural confidence, for me, means taking Chinese society seriously in its complexity — rural and urban, ordinary work and high policy — and serving from that seriousness.
