# City / cross-boundary leadership programme — gain & contribution

**Context cues:** Ho Chi Minh City and Kuala Lumpur mentioned; structured debriefing; cultural intelligence.

---

## What do you hope to gain through your participation in the programme?

I hope to gain a tested form of leadership that can survive contact with reality. Much of my academic work sits at the intersection of climate, urban life, and health risk. Yet the decisions that shape a city are rarely made at the clean edges of a discipline. They are made across boundaries, where incentives compete, where priorities collide, and where power is distributed unevenly. I am applying to the programme because it offers a structured environment to practise leading in precisely that terrain, rather than only describing it.

First, I want to develop cultural intelligence that is more than awareness. The programme’s sequence, from preparatory learning to immersion and organizational attachment, creates a progression from concept to consequence. I want to become faster at reading context, more precise in communication when assumptions do not match, and more adaptable in style without compromising standards. In unfamiliar environments, small misunderstandings compound quickly. I want to learn how to prevent that compounding, and how to repair trust when it occurs.

Second, I hope to gain a working knowledge of how city systems actually move. Ho Chi Minh City and Kuala Lumpur are both dynamic hubs where growth, inequality, infrastructure, and opportunity meet at speed. I want to see the mechanisms beneath the headlines: how organizations coordinate across sectors, how constraints shape what is possible, and where leverage points emerge. I am especially interested in how data is interpreted and used at the operational level, where technical evidence must compete with budget, politics, and human attention.

Third, I want to sharpen a leadership practice rooted in reflection and accountability. The programme’s emphasis on structured debriefing matters to me because experiences do not automatically become learning. I want to return to HKU with a clearer understanding of how I lead under pressure, what I avoid when uncertainty rises, and how I can translate insight into repeatable habits. My goal is not a memorable trip. My goal is a durable upgrade in how I work with people across difference to deliver outcomes that matter.

## How do you think you would contribute to the breadth of perspective and dynamics of the group?

I would contribute a bridge between analytical rigor and human reality, and I would do so in a way that strengthens both the quality of thinking and the quality of collaboration. I have trained in technical environments where claims must be defensible, models must be interrogated, and uncertainty must be named. I have also chosen a path in global health and policy because the point of evidence is not elegance. The point is protection. In a diverse cohort, I can help the group translate between different ways of seeing, so that disagreement becomes productive rather than personal.

I bring a systems perspective that is practical rather than abstract. When teams struggle, it is often because they treat a complex situation as a simple argument. I tend to reframe problems into assumptions that can be tested, stakeholders that can be mapped, and constraints that can be worked with. This makes it easier for a group to move from opinion to strategy, and from strategy to action. At the same time, I am attentive to what data cannot capture: trust, dignity, fear, and the quiet pressures that shape whether people participate fully.

I would also contribute momentum and structure. In group settings, I naturally move toward clarifying roles, aligning on timelines, and creating checkpoints that prevent drift. Structure, however, is not control. It is care for the shared task. I try to create conditions where quieter voices shape decisions, where credit is shared, and where accountability is mutual. I am comfortable with friction and debate, but I am disciplined about listening, because cross-boundary leadership fails when confidence substitutes for understanding.

If selected, I would aim to be the participant who raises the standard without raising the temperature: someone who helps the group think more clearly, act more deliberately, and leave the programme not only with stories, but with results and a stronger capacity to work across boundaries.
