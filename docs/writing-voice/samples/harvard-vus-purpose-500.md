# Harvard VUS — purpose statement (~500 words)

**Prompt:** Please upload a statement explaining your purpose in studying at Harvard as a Visiting Undergraduate Student… background… non-academic interests… benefit/contribute outside classroom… (~500 words)

---

In Berlin last year, sitting in the back of a plenary at the World Health Summit, I noticed a quiet, unsettling geography: the people closest to the communities carried the most precise stories, while the people closest to the capital held the levers that decide which stories become policy. The distance between the microphone and the decision-maker was only ten meters, yet the signal loss was total. I left with a clear discomfort. I realized that while my medical education has been rigorous, it has been anchored on only one side of this room.

Currently, I am anchored in implementation. Through my fieldwork in rural Gannan and my clinical studies at HKU, I have learned to respect the “messy middle” of delivery—where logistics, trust, and workforce constraints determine if care reaches the patient. I can see how programs succeed or fail on the ground, but I lack the anchor of design. I need to understand the theoretical architecture of why the system is built this way. That is my purpose in applying to Harvard: to study the design logic of global health directly from its primary architects.

My academic plan is structured around a deliberate tension. I intend to place The Political Economy of Globalization in direct dialogue with Who Lives, Who Dies. To be effective in policy, I must understand the macro-economic engine of the world order as defined by Lawrence Summers. Yet, I must simultaneously interrogate that engine through the lens of Salmaan Keshavjee’s Social Medicine, which reveals how those very economic choices manifest as “structural violence” in the bodies of the poor. I am seeking the friction between these two worldviews; the solution to global health inequity lies in the negotiation between the economist’s logic and the anthropologist’s conscience.

I will bridge this divide with the behavioral pragmatism of Cass Sunstein, learning the “architecture of choice” to translate high-level goals into adoption. Finally, I will turn inward with Michael Puett’s Classical Chinese Ethical and Political Theory. I plan to ground this inquiry in the Harvard-Yenching Library, utilizing its archives to compare historical Chinese models of social governance with the Western frameworks I learn in class. My goal is epistemological translation: by studying how Western academia decodes Eastern philosophy, I can train myself to better translate Asian governance models for Western policy rooms.

Outside the classroom, I engage with the world through two disciplines that demand opposite forms of focus: the court and the brush. At St. John’s College, I play on the basketball team because the court is one of the few places where intellectual posturing dissolves. You cannot debate a pick-and-roll; you must simply execute it. I have found that the quickest way to build trust in a new environment is not through conversation, but through the non-verbal geometry of the game. I hope to bring this energy to the intramural leagues at Harvard—using the universal grammar of the sport to break down the silos between visiting students and the residential houses.

Conversely, I practice Chinese calligraphy to learn the discipline of irreversibility. In medicine, we can often adjust a treatment; in policy, we can amend a law. But on rice paper, once the brush touches the surface, the ink spreads instantly. There is no “undo.” This art form forces a terrifying mindfulness; it reminds me that every stroke—like every policy intervention—carries a weight that cannot be erased, only acknowledged. I hope to share this practice, perhaps through the Harvard-Radcliffe Chinese Students Association, not just as a cultural artifact, but as a training in decisiveness.

I am not applying to Harvard VUS to collect a brand. I am applying because I need to return to that room in Berlin. Next time, however, I do not want to sit in the back, observing the gap between the story and the lever. I want to arrive equipped with the architectural knowledge to rearrange the room, ensuring that the signal no longer dies in the distance between them.
