How can a scholar be defined?

To me, a scholar is a bridge. They stand in the gap between the abstraction of data and the messy reality of human life. My career is an attempt to build that bridge. I aspire to become a policy-focused epidemiologist, translating complex environmental data into actionable urban legislation that protects vulnerable populations.

For years, I leaned on the abstract. Through the Quantum Computing for Gifted Students Scheme and atmospheric modeling at the Hong Kong Observatory, I learned to model complex systems. But while physics explains how the universe works, it rarely intervenes in how a person lives. Data describes the world, but it takes humanity to change it.

I crossed the bridge to Global Health to apply these rigorous tools to humanity. In a program spanning law, medicine, and social science courses, my 4.24 GPA evidences a fluency across disciplines.

But a scholar cannot live in a library. As a member of the St. John’s College basketball team, winning the interhall basketball championship required the same discipline as my research—grit, strategy, and the ability to endure friction until the breakthrough comes. I was surrounded by talented peers across all faculties, and I see the Laidlaw community as a global expansion of this environment. I am applying because Laidlaw is unique in demanding this dual growth: it funds the rigorous research I need to hone my technical skills, while simultaneously forcing me out of the lab to practice leadership in the real world.

This blend of intellectual rigor and social resilience drives my work. As a Youth Fellow of the MIT Hong Kong Innovation Node, I founded MEDocGPT to make healthcare consultancy accessible to the underserved. This was my first step as a pracademic—acquiring the eye to see the invisible and the hands to build a solution.

My proposed research synthesizes my interests in climate, urbanism, and health. By challenging the historical consensus that cold weather is the primary driver of heart and stroke admissions, I will investigate whether a warming, aging Hong Kong has reached a tipping point toward heat-related risks. This project bridges my past and future, allowing me to operationalize my theoretical training into applied epidemiological skills necessary to inform actionable health policy via environmental data.

Leadership is the act of connecting people. As a Forum Ambassador at the Hong Kong Laureate Forum and delegate at the World Health Summit, I realized that the best solutions come from the collision of perspectives. Through my Leadership-in-Action project, I seek to expand this dialogue to Medellin, Colombia. By joining the “Peace and Social Resilience” project with make_sense, I intend to facilitate a mutual exchange, sharing Hong Kong’s insights on high-density urban health while absorbing Medellin’s strategies for community resilience.

Convention defines scholars by specialization; I believe the opposite. A true scholar is driven by a relentless curiosity that refuses boundaries. I am ready to be a Laidlaw Scholar.
