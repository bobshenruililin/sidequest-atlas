## Please outline your motivation, intention and interest for attending NEWDAY (max 500 words)

I am applying to NEWDAY because I believe the most critical skill for the next generation of leadership is not the ability to win an argument, but the capacity to sustain a dialogue.

As a global health student, my training is often binary: diagnosis is either correct or incorrect; treatment either works or fails. However, the context in which health is delivered is rarely so clean. It is messy, political, and deeply human. I have found that technical expertise is insufficient without the diplomatic capacity to navigate cultural and ideological divides. I am drawn to the Nansen Academy because it represents a lineage of thought that bridges these worlds. Fridtjof Nansen himself moved from the precision of oceanography to the complexity of humanitarianism. I see my own path mirroring this trajectory—moving from the hard science of medicine to the soft power of policy and peace-building.

My motivation is specifically rooted in the “East-West” dimension of the academy. Hong Kong sits at the fault line of this geopolitical shift. I live in a city where different systems of governance, law, and philosophy converge—and occasionally collide. I want to attend NEWDAY to step outside this immediate friction and view it through the lens of the Nordic model. I am interested in how the Nordic tradition of consensus-building can be adapted to the sharper, faster-paced dynamics of East Asia.

I intend to use this program to test a specific hypothesis: that the principles of medical ethics—beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice—can serve as a universal grammar for cross-cultural dialogue. When political language fails, the language of human vulnerability often remains intelligible.

I am not looking for a passive lecture series. I am seeking a rigorous intellectual environment where my assumptions about the West are challenged by Nordic scholars, and where I can contribute a nuanced perspective on the East that goes beyond headlines. I want to leave Lillehammer with a tested framework for dialogue that can survive contact with the polarized reality I will return to.

## What would you say are the most burning issues of our time? (max 500 words)

The most burning issue of our time is not a specific crisis—be it climate, conflict, or contagion—but the asymmetry between our shared threats and our fractured trust.

We are currently living through a polycrisis, where risks are networked but our solutions remain siloed. The defining characteristic of this era is that our problems respect no borders, while our politics are becoming increasingly walled off.

First, we face an epistemological crisis. Before we can solve climate change or regulate artificial intelligence, we must agree on a baseline of reality. In both the East and the West, information ecosystems have fragmented to the point where we no longer share a common set of facts. When truth becomes tribal, cooperation becomes impossible. This is a public health emergency as much as a political one; we saw during the pandemic that scientific evidence cannot save lives if the social fabric is too torn to accept it.

Second, we are struggling with the lag between technological velocity and ethical capacity. We are deploying technologies—from generative AI to genomic editing—that have the power to reshape what it means to be human. Yet, our ethical frameworks and regulatory bodies operate on 20th-century timelines. We are building the engine faster than we are building the steering wheel. The question of how we govern these technologies requires a dialogue that spans the philosophical traditions of both East—which often prioritizes collective stability—and West—which often prioritizes individual liberty.

Finally, there is the issue of intergenerational justice. Decisions made today regarding carbon emissions and debt accumulation are effectively a tax levied on a generation that has no seat at the negotiating table.

I believe the role of our generation is to rebuild the connective tissue of the global order. The burning issue is not the scarcity of resources, but the scarcity of trust required to manage them. We have the technical solutions to many of our problems; what we lack is the diplomatic architecture to implement them equitably. Addressing this requires leaders who are bilingual not just in language, but in culture—leaders who can translate the anxieties of one side into the aspirations of the other.
