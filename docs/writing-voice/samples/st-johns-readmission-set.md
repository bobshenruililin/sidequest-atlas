## Did you participate in any teams, clubs, and activities within and outside of St. John’s this year (2025 – 2026)? Please specify your role (especially leadership, if any).

Within St. John's this year, I played with the Basketball Team, took part in the Theatre Club, and helped with the DaVinci meets DaVinci exhibition alongside our alumna artist Michelle Wong — an experience that quietly became one of the most meaningful of my year, because it reminded me that St. John's is not bound by the walls of a single academic cycle. It is a lineage.

Outside of the Hall, the year demanded that I step into rooms I never imagined I would occupy. I served as a Forum Ambassador at the HK Laureate Forum and represented Hong Kong at the World Health Summit in Berlin. I have been selected as the Hong Kong Representative for the upcoming APEC Voices of the Future in Shenzhen. As a Ting Ka Ping Foundation Scholar, I undertook a field trip to Gannan, and on the strength of that work I was appointed Student Coordinator for Wu Zhi Qiao, leading a service initiative in Matcha Village, Gansu. Academically, I have held a 4.24 GPA — the highest in the history of my degree program — and was selected as the sole Laidlaw Scholar from my cohort, conducting research under Professor David Bishai, Director of the School of Public Health. I secured academic sponsorship from the Nordic Council of Ministers for the Nansen East-West Dialogue Academy, which will take me to Lillehammer, Stockholm, and Helsinki this summer.

I name these not to impress, but to be honest about where my year went — and, more importantly, about where I now wish to bring it back.

## What are your plans for the coming year (2026 – 2027)? Will you continue to join the same teams, clubs, and activities? Why or why not?

Yes — and more. I intend to continue with the Basketball Team and the Theatre Club, and to widen my physical commitment to the Hall by joining the Soccer Team and the Lacrosse Team. The reason I can commit more next year, rather than less, is a matter of architecture: my global year has already been built, and its travel now falls cleanly outside term. My Laidlaw conference takes place in London during reading week; my leadership element will be completed in Shanghai; and my summer will be spent in Medellín, Colombia, with the NGO make_sense. The rest of my summers, through Laidlaw, are also accounted for.

What this means, practically, is that the frantic front-loading of this past year is over. My external life has settled into its rhythm, and term-time is finally free to belong to St. John's — not as a guest between flights, but as a resident. I am coming back to sleep in my own room, to walk into the canteen at hours that matter, and to show up on courts and stages with the consistency I owed the Hall long before now.

## How well do you think you have integrated into the community of St. John’s so far? Describe your experience and share a moment/feature/culture you enjoy the most or the least during your time here.

If I tell you I have integrated well, I will be lying, and St. John's deserves better than that from me. The truth is that my integration this year was fractured, and I carry some responsibility for it. I began the year active and present, but as the weight of external commitments and the intense demands of my research grew, I started retreating. I live close to campus, and that proximity became my undoing: an evening home turned into a week home, and a week home turned into a quiet, private exile. It was escapism, dressed up as logistics. I was overwhelmed by the intensity of hall socialisation and I let my absence explain itself.

The lowest point came when I returned to find my room had been repurposed for affay orientation in my absence, leaving me with the quiet, sad realization of just how far I had slipped out of the Hall's daily consciousness. I swallowed it, because in that moment I did not know which channel was mine to speak through. I felt voiceless — a small, specific kind of voicelessness that belongs to people who feel their efforts are not being met with understanding on the other side. What restored me was not a formal process but a human one: the floor chairs and vice-chairs sat down with me, and they listened. They understood. That conversation did not erase what had happened, but it reminded me that the grace of this community is real, and that it extends even to those of us who have drifted.

And in the drifting, I found that I had integrated after all — just through a different door. The most formative moments of my year at St. John's were the sherry sessions after High Table, where alumni spoke to me as though I were already one of them; the near-daily conversations I have had with our Chaplain and former Master, Rev. Dr. Eric Chong; and the afternoons spent helping Michelle Wong with her exhibition, listening to a St. Johnian tell me, through her art, what the Hall had meant to her decades after she'd left it. This is the St. John's I love most — the one that persists past any single cohort, the one that folds you in intellectually and spiritually even when you have failed to fold yourself in physically. It is the part of the College I want to spend next year returning to properly, and repaying.

## If you could change/ improve one thing in St. John’s, what would that be? What does it need to bring about such change and how would you play a role in it?

For the first time since 2017, St. John's is not first in GPA. The hall average has fallen to 3.24. I say this not as a critic, but as someone who has spent the last year deeply navigating the extreme demands of academic and scholarly excellence, and who has thought about this tension more carefully than I would like to admit.

The thing I would most want to change is the unspoken tension between the lifestyle that makes St. John's socially extraordinary and the lifestyle that global academic excellence increasingly demands. I do not want to be blunt, but I have to be honest: the rhythms that produce Laidlaw Scholars, APEC representatives, and top-tier academic standing are, at a fundamental level, often at odds with the rhythms that produce the intense social fabric we are known for. Pretending otherwise is part of what has brought us here.

The change I hope to see is not a retreat from our culture but an expansion of it. St. John's does not need to sacrifice who it is; it needs to widen the range of what a successful St. Johnian is allowed to look like. A hall-mate deep in a research cycle, or preparing a delegation paper, or writing into the early hours should feel as visibly supported and as culturally celebrated as our sports captains and our orientation leaders. We need to make it normal — not noble, not heroic, just normal — to be in an academic-building season and still belong entirely.

My role in this, if I am readmitted, would not be to lecture anyone on how to study. It would be to be present. To be the Martin Scholar who shows up to basketball practice. To be the Laidlaw Scholar who sits at High Table and then at siu. To make the two halves of the Hall's life legible to each other, by simply embodying both. And to lean, always, on what I have come to believe is St. John's greatest and least-discussed asset: its alumni. The resilience of this College has never lived in its rankings. It has lived in the people who keep coming back — to mentor, to exhibit, to pour sherry, to talk past midnight. That is why, even after a fractured year, I believe St. John's will bounce back. And it is why, despite everything that might sound in this application like a critique, I am asking, with both hands, to come home.
