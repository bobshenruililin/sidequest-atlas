## What is your favorite TED or TEDx talk? Why?

My favorite talk is Gabe Whaley’s "This TED Talk Is Full of Bad Ideas" because it fundamentally reframes the relationship between absurdity and profound connection. Whaley demonstrates that nothing is ever quite what it appears to be on the surface; a shared car key becomes a catalyst for community, and a judgmental ATM transforms into a celebration of solidarity. This resonates deeply with me because it challenges the paralysis of perfectionism. It serves as a reminder that "bad" ideas are not dead ends, but often the necessary vehicles we must ride to arrive at truly innovative and human experiences. I strive to embody the philosophy of "expecting the unexpected" that Whaley champions throughout the talk. His stories prove that when we strip away the fear of being wrong or looking foolish, we create space for genuine surprise. Whether it is the chaotic joy of the Big Red Boots or the empathy found in a leaderboard of bank balances, the talk illustrates that the true value of an idea isn't in its initial logic, but in the reaction it provokes. For me, this talk is a personal manifesto to remain curious, to engage with the things that make me uncomfortable, and to trust that the most meaningful outcomes often hide behind the ideas we are quickest to dismiss.

## If you are to create a TEDx talk about any subject you are interested in, what would that be? How would that TEDx talk be like?

If I were to give a TEDx talk, I would do it on Doraemon. On the surface, the show appears to be about a robot cat with a pocket full of miracles, but I believe the true protagonist is actually the failure of those miracles. Every episode follows a "bad idea" structure: Nobita seeks a shortcut, Doraemon provides a gadget, and the situation inevitably spirals into unexpected chaos. Rather than analyzing the logic of the gadgets, I want to explore the social dynamic that allows this cycle to continue. The show teaches us that a community isn't built on perfection or success; it is built on the messy, hilarious, and forgiving interactions between deeply flawed individuals. I would argue that we need to embody the optimism found in this chaotic ecosystem. In our adult lives, we often fear the "Gian" (the bully) or the "Suneo" (the cynic), and we hide our inner "Nobita" (the clumsy failure). However, Doraemon presents a world where these social frictions don't lead to isolation, but to a strange, resilient bond. The most profound lesson isn't in the solution the gadget provides, but in the fact that Doraemon stays. He represents a kindness that doesn't demand competence as a prerequisite for love. By embracing the fun and the folly of Doraemon, we learn that "bad ideas" and personal shortcomings are often the very things that draw us closer together, provided we have the courage to laugh at them.

## What makes a good speech?

A good speech is defined by its ability to create a fundamental shift in the audience’s perspective, effectively altering how they process information rather than simply telling them what to think. This quality, often described as leverage, ensures that the insight shared generates a lasting impact on the listener's worldview that persists well beyond the duration of the address. It requires the speaker to identify the discrepancy between the current reality and a potential future, guiding the audience through that transition with logic and emotional intelligence. Beyond the traditional rhetorical elements of credibility, emotion, and logic, profound speaking relies on the maturity to prioritize the audience over the speaker’s ego. This involves a deliberate removal of performative artifice to establish genuine resonance. When a speaker focuses on serving the truth of the subject matter rather than seeking validation, they foster a sense of trust and openness. This vulnerability allows the message to be internalized, transforming the interaction from a monologue into a mutual recognition of shared experience. Finally, the value of a speech is contingent upon its alignment with its ultimate purpose, whether that is to incite action or to offer solace. Content not intended for strategic advantage derives its worth from its capacity to validate the human condition and articulate complex emotions. A successful speaker possesses the situational awareness to discern whether the moment requires a call to change or simply a testament to reality, ensuring that the content respects the specific context and needs of those listening.

## Why do you want to join TEDxHKU?

I want to join TEDxHKU because I believe the TED format is the most effective training ground for my future as a policymaker. As a Global Health and Development student, I have learned that data alone does not change the world; the communication of that data does. My goal is to master the art of distilling complex, global issues into their most powerful, accessible forms, and TED is the gold standard for this specific type of advocacy. My background is defined by what I call "diffusion thinking"—the ability to see intersections where others see boundaries. On paper, my experience seems contradictory: I had conducted atmospheric research at the Hong Kong Observatory and quantum physics research at HKUST, yet I am also a Hong Kong Young Writers' Award category winner and a World Scholar’s Cup Champion Debater. However, these are not separate interests; they are evidence of my ability to synthesize the analytical rigor of science with the persuasive power of the humanities. I recently put this into practice during my BASC9001 final project, where I had to integrate diverse disciplinary perspectives into a coherent, 6-minute narrative. That experience confirmed that my happy place is exactly where TEDxHKU operates: at the convergence of disparate ideas. I want to join the team to apply my ability to bridge disciplines, to contribute my energy as a "source of ideas," and to help curate a platform where the next generation of changemakers can be heard.
